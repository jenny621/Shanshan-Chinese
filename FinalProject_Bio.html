About Me Professionally

Currently I am a doctoral student at The Ohio State University. I have been teaching Mandarin Chinese at U.S. universities for over 6 years. I take great pains to craft contextualized lesson plans and class activities for my students, because contextual factors like time, place, who you're talking to, who else is present, and so on, play crucial roles in determining what we say and how we say it. Here is an example. Saying "Ni pang le! (You've gotten fat!)" to a male Chinese-speaking friend or acquaintance after not seeing him for a while shows your care for the person, with an undertone of "You've gotten lucky (because you've been eating well)." However, saying the same phrase to a Chinese-speaking female is becoming less common nowadays due to the changing body beauty standards for women.

Because of this insistence on creating authentic contexts for my students, I have been experimenting with using virtual reality (VR) to create contextualized and embodied learning experiences—be it in a Taiwanese breakfast shop or at a train station at Shanghai—so my students can just "be there" to explore and interpret the meaning of a sentence by taking into account all the relevant contextual information. I am currently developing a Taiwanese kitchen scene in VR where the user can learn to use Mandarin Chinese to cook scrambled eggs and tomatoes with a friend or an avatar.
